About

I am a PhD student in astronomy at Johns Hopkins University and a NASA FINESST recipient. I use ground and space-based telescopes to study star and planet formation. I aim to understand how planetary systems form and evolve and to place our solar system in context with others. I have a particular focus on debris disk systems which are analogous to our Kuiper Belt and are host to planets, smaller bodies like comets, and gas and dust. The composition of bodies in these systems as well as their structure and dynamics hold information on how planetary systems come to be. I use facilities like JWST, ALMA, VLT, etc., to study these exoplanetary systems. I always welcome new collaborations, so please reach out if there is anything you wish to discuss..

Research

My research focuses on understanding star and planet formation using observations across the electromagnetic spectrum from near-infrared to radio wavelengths. I have highlighted some of my projects below.

1. As a part of JWST GO program 1563 along with my collaborators, I used the NIRSpec IFU to study the gas-rich 49 Ceti debris disk. I developed high-contrast imaging techniques for the NIRSpec IFU to spatially resolve fluorescently excited CO emission in a debris disk. The CO emission can be explained by a model that considers fluorescent pumping from the central star. We compared the constraints from this fluorescent model to predictions for primordial and secondary gas to try and determine the origin of the gas in this system. This discovery offers a new probe into the mysterious nature of gas in debris disks. Link to this paper: Link

2. For JWST GTO program 1294, I analyzed MIRI MRS data of the Beta Pictoris system. I developed high-contrast imaging techniques for the MIRI MRS and used them to detect spatially resolved warm dust, argon gas emission, and to extract the first mid-IR spectrum of the planet Beta Pic b. We find that the atmospheric composition of Beta Pic b is generally consistent with predictions of a core accretion formation mechanism with atmospheric pollution. The discovery of argon gas suggests that the planetesimals in Beta Pic were formed in a cold region of the disk. Link to this paper: Link

3. My collaborators and I used the VLA to search for carbon chain molecules in a sample of 12 molecular cloud clumps. We detected carbon chain molecules in 11 of the sources and used their abundances compared to chemistry models to determine a chemical age on the clumps. We find that the clumps are likely less than 1 Myr in age and are likely starless because they have not had sufficient time to form high-mass stars. Link to this paper: Link

4. My collaborators and I used the NASA IRTF to search for CO absorption along the line of sight to the HD 32297 debris disk. We did not detect CO absorption and used the upper limit to infer the vertical distribution of the gas in the system. Link to the paper: Link
